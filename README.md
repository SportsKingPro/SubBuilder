# SubBuilder
Welcome to the DFW Regional Metroplex Simulation Engine. In this game you take control of a growing transit agency responsible for designing, expanding, and managing a regional rail network. Your goal is to connect neighborhoods efficiently, attract more riders, maintain a healthy budget, and gradually transform the metroplex into a world-class transportation system. Every decision affects passenger behavior, operating costs, revenue, and long-term growth, so balancing expansion with financial stability is essential.

Begin a new game from the main menu by selecting an available map. Each map contains different neighborhoods, population densities, and travel patterns that create unique challenges. Once the simulation loads, time begins moving automatically and commuters immediately begin traveling between residential, commercial, and recreational destinations. As the transit authority, you must build a network capable of serving these trips while staying within your available budget.

The map is your primary workspace. You can pan around the region, zoom in for detailed construction, or zoom out to understand the broader network. The minimap provides a quick overview of your entire system and allows you to quickly navigate to distant sections of the city. As your network expands, learning to move efficiently around the map becomes increasingly important.

The sidebar contains nearly every management tool available in the simulation. Each section can be expanded or collapsed to organize information and reduce clutter. Here you will find construction tools, financial reports, network statistics, objectives, commuter information, and operational controls. As your transit system becomes more complex, these management panels become essential for monitoring performance and planning future expansion.

Construction begins by selecting a transit line color and placing stations on the map. Stations can be connected to form complete rail lines that passengers use to travel between destinations. Building stations closer to high-density neighborhoods increases potential ridership, while thoughtful routing reduces travel times and encourages more commuters to choose public transportation over driving.

Each transit line operates independently and has its own statistics. The Network panel displays ridership, capacity, revenue, operating costs, efficiency, and station performance for every line. Reviewing these statistics regularly helps identify overcrowded routes, underused stations, and opportunities for future expansion. Successful transit systems continuously evolve based on passenger demand.

Stations are more than simple stops. Selecting a station opens the Station Inspector, where you can view passenger activity, capacity, connected lines, and available upgrades. Investing in station improvements increases comfort, efficiency, and long-term ridership. Some upgrades reduce congestion while others improve passenger satisfaction or increase station capacity, making strategic investment an important part of network management.

Neighborhoods generate commuters with unique travel preferences and daily routines. Selecting a neighborhood opens the AI Commuter Inspector, where you can examine individual travelers and understand how they choose between driving, walking, and public transportation. Every commuter evaluates travel time, convenience, cost, and available routes before making a transportation decision. Improving your network changes these decisions over time, gradually shifting more travelers toward transit.

The economy system governs every major decision. Building infrastructure requires capital, while operating transit lines generates recurring expenses. Fare revenue depends on passenger usage, so expanding too quickly may create financial strain before enough riders begin using the system. Maintaining a positive cash flow while continuing to improve service is one of the primary challenges throughout the simulation.

Objectives provide structured goals that introduce new mechanics while rewarding efficient management. Completing objectives grants financial bonuses and unlocks additional opportunities for expansion. Objectives also serve as a guide for newer players by encouraging balanced development instead of uncontrolled growth.

Simulation controls allow you to pause the game, adjust simulation speed, and monitor the current time. Slowing the simulation is useful when designing complicated rail networks, while faster speeds allow you to observe long-term trends in ridership, finances, and commuter behavior. Periodically pausing to evaluate your network often leads to better planning decisions.

The save system allows you to preserve your progress at any point during the simulation. Multiple save slots let you maintain different cities or experiment with alternate development strategies without losing previous work. Autosaves provide protection against accidental mistakes, while manual saves allow you to create milestones before major construction projects. Loading a save restores your network, finances, commuter behavior, objectives, upgrades, and overall simulation state exactly as it was when the save was created.

Success comes from building a transit network that is both financially sustainable and attractive to commuters. Expanding strategically, monitoring passenger demand, investing in station improvements, and responding to changing travel patterns will steadily increase ridership and revenue. With careful planning, your small transit agency can grow into a highly efficient regional transportation network that connects the entire metroplex.
