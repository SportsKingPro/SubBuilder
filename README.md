# SubBuilder
Tutorial Part 1: Welcome to the Metroplex

Welcome to Metro Simulation.

In this game, you are responsible for planning, building, and operating a regional rail system across the Dallas Fort Worth metropolitan area. Every station you place, every rail line you build, and every dollar you spend will shape how millions of people travel throughout the region.

Unlike many transportation games, commuters do not automatically use your network. Every resident makes independent decisions based on where they live, where they work, how long each trip takes, how much it costs, how far they must walk, and how convenient your rail system is compared to driving.

Your goal is not simply to build rail lines. Your goal is to build a network that people actually want to use.

At the beginning of the game, almost everyone travels by car because no transit network exists. Your job is to slowly change that by building a system that is faster, more convenient, and financially sustainable.

The Dallas Fort Worth region contains dozens of major employment centers, universities, airports, shopping districts, entertainment destinations, and residential communities. Some areas generate enormous numbers of daily trips while others have relatively little demand. Learning where people want to travel is one of the most important skills in the game.

Before building anything, spend a few moments exploring the map.

Zoom in and out using the mouse wheel.

Click and drag to move around the region.

The minimap allows you to quickly jump between different parts of the metroplex.

As you explore, notice the locations of downtowns, airports, universities, and large commercial centers. These destinations typically generate much more passenger demand than low density suburban neighborhoods.

You should also remember that building infrastructure is expensive.

Every station costs money to construct.

Every rail line costs money to maintain.

Every train increases your operating expenses.

Running out of money will prevent you from expanding your network, so careful planning is just as important as good engineering.

This simulation rewards thoughtful decisions instead of rapid expansion. A small network connecting important destinations is usually far more successful than a large network serving places with little demand.

When you are ready, continue to the next tutorial to learn how to place your first station.

Tutorial Part 2: Building Your First Line

Now that you understand how the simulation works, it is time to build your first rail line.

The first decision you will make is where to place your stations. A station serves the area surrounding it, allowing nearby residents and destinations to access your network. Stations placed in locations with high population, employment, shopping, or entertainment will generally attract more riders than stations in low density areas.

Select the Station tool and click on the map to place your first station.

Good starting locations include downtown districts, airports, universities, major shopping centers, and large employment hubs. These areas generate thousands of daily trips and provide a strong foundation for your network.

After placing your first station, place a second station in another major destination. Rail lines become much more useful when they connect places that people frequently travel between.

Avoid placing stations too close together. Their coverage areas will overlap, increasing construction and maintenance costs without significantly increasing ridership. Likewise, avoid placing stations too far apart. If commuters must walk a long distance to reach a station, many will simply choose to drive instead.

Once two or more stations have been built, select the Line tool.

Choose the color you want your line to use. Each color represents a different rail service and helps distinguish routes throughout your network.

Click each station in the order you want trains to visit them. The order matters because trains will follow this exact route during operation.

Take a moment before confirming your line. Consider whether the route makes sense. Does it connect important destinations? Will commuters actually travel between these locations? Could the line be extended later without major changes?

After your line has been created, trains can begin operating.

Every line requires operating costs. Longer routes generally require more trains, consume more resources, and increase maintenance expenses. During the early game, shorter lines connecting important destinations are usually more profitable than long routes serving many low demand areas.

You should also think about future expansion. A successful first line often becomes the backbone of a much larger regional network. Building with future extensions in mind can save both money and time later in the game.

Congratulations. You have now built your first rail corridor. In the next tutorial you will learn how passengers choose between driving and transit, why some stations attract more riders than others, and how to increase ridership across your entire network.

Tutorial Part 3: Understanding Your Passengers

Your rail network is now operating, but building tracks does not guarantee passengers.

Every commuter in the simulation decides how to travel based on several factors. Some people will continue driving, while others may choose transit if your network offers a better trip. Your goal is to make rail the most attractive option whenever possible.

The most important factor is travel time.

Passengers compare how long it takes to drive with how long it takes to use your rail system. A trip that requires long walks, multiple transfers, or lengthy waits is much less appealing than one with a fast and direct train ride.

Walking distance also plays a major role.

Stations only serve people within their coverage area. If homes, offices, schools, or businesses are located too far away, commuters may never consider using transit. Expanding your network into areas with high population or employment will generally increase ridership more than adding stations in low demand locations.

Service frequency is equally important.

Passengers prefer trains that arrive often. A line with frequent service reduces waiting time and makes the entire network feel more convenient. However, increasing frequency also increases operating costs because additional trains must be placed into service. Finding the right balance between convenience and cost is one of the most important management decisions you will make.

Transfers can also influence travel choices.

A direct trip is usually preferred over one that requires changing trains. While transfers allow you to build larger and more efficient networks, too many transfers can make transit less attractive. Whenever possible, connect major destinations with simple and logical routes.

Station congestion affects passenger experience as well.

As more riders use a station, platforms become busier. If passenger demand exceeds the station's capacity, delays and overcrowding begin to reduce the quality of service. Upgrading heavily used stations before they become overloaded will help maintain reliable operations.

Do not judge your network only by its size.

A network with twenty poorly placed stations may carry fewer passengers than one with six stations located in strategic locations. Successful transit agencies focus on connecting places where people actually need to travel instead of trying to cover every neighborhood immediately.

Use the information panels to monitor your system.

Station statistics show how many passengers are using each station, whether it is approaching capacity, and what upgrades may be beneficial. Line statistics reveal which routes are carrying the most riders and whether service levels should be adjusted. Neighborhood information can help identify areas with strong demand that are not yet connected to your network.

As your city grows, travel patterns will continue to evolve. A line that performs well today may require additional capacity later, while new developments may create entirely new travel corridors. Continue monitoring your network and adapt your system as demand changes.

Understanding why commuters make their decisions is the key to building a successful transit system. In the next tutorial, you will learn how to improve stations, increase capacity, and use upgrades to attract even more passengers.

Tutorial Part 4: Growing Your Network

Congratulations. Your first rail line is operating and passengers are beginning to use your system. As demand increases, your focus will shift from simply building infrastructure to expanding and improving your network.

One of the biggest mistakes new transit planners make is expanding too quickly. Every new station, line, and train increases your operating expenses. If you build faster than your revenue grows, your agency can quickly begin losing money.

Before constructing a new line, evaluate how your existing network is performing.

Open the Lines and Networks panel to compare each route. Look for lines with strong ridership, crowded stations, or consistently high passenger volumes. These routes may benefit from additional trains, station upgrades, or extensions to nearby destinations.

Stations should also be monitored regularly.

A busy station is usually a sign that the surrounding area generates strong travel demand. Instead of immediately building somewhere new, consider investing in that station first. Expanding capacity and improving passenger facilities often provides a greater return than constructing another station in a low demand area.

Many stations can also become transfer points.

Transfer stations allow passengers to move between multiple rail lines without leaving the system. A well placed transfer station can dramatically increase the usefulness of your network because it connects several destinations together. However, too many transfer stations can create unnecessary complexity, so they should be placed carefully.

As your network expands, think about how each line serves a specific purpose.

Some lines should connect major employment centers.

Others may link residential neighborhoods with downtown districts.

Some routes are best suited for airports, universities, or entertainment areas.

Giving every line a clear purpose usually creates a network that is easier for passengers to understand and more efficient to operate.

Do not feel obligated to connect every station directly.

Passengers are willing to transfer when it saves time. Building several interconnected lines is often more effective than creating one extremely long route that attempts to serve every destination.

Your budget should always influence your decisions.

If revenue is increasing steadily, you can safely begin expanding into new areas.

If revenue begins falling, pause expansion and improve your existing network instead. Increasing ridership on current lines is often less expensive than constructing entirely new ones.

Objectives can also guide your expansion.

Completing objectives rewards additional funding that can be invested back into your agency. If you are unsure what to build next, reviewing your current objectives is often a good place to start.

Remember that transit systems are never truly finished.

Cities grow, travel patterns change, and passenger demand shifts over time. Continue observing how commuters use your network and adapt your rail system as the metropolitan area evolves.

In the next tutorial, you will learn how station upgrades, policies, and advanced management tools can dramatically improve ridership, efficiency, and the long term success of your transit agency.

Tutorial Part 5: Mastering the Metroplex

By this point you have learned how to build stations, create rail lines, and manage your growing network. The next step is learning how to think like a real transit planner.

Every expansion should answer a simple question. Why does this project need to exist?

The best rail lines solve transportation problems. They connect places where large numbers of people already travel every day. Before building a new station, consider who will use it, where those passengers are going, and whether the investment will improve your entire network.

As your agency grows, you will unlock additional tools that allow you to influence how commuters travel.

Station upgrades can increase capacity, improve passenger comfort, expand accessibility, or make stations more attractive destinations. Some upgrades are best suited for busy downtown stations, while others are designed for suburban communities where commuters may drive to the station before boarding a train.

Regional policies also become increasingly important.

Policies affect the entire transportation system rather than a single station or rail line. Some policies encourage transit use, while others improve operations or increase revenue. Every policy has advantages and disadvantages, so choosing the right combination depends on the current needs of your network.

Your statistics panels provide valuable information about how your system is performing.

Review your lines regularly to identify routes with the highest ridership. Examine individual stations to determine which locations are becoming overcrowded. Neighborhood information can reveal areas with strong travel demand that are not yet connected to your network.

Do not ignore stations with low ridership.

A poorly performing station does not always mean it was placed incorrectly. It may simply need a better connection to the rest of the network, more frequent service, or nearby destinations that attract additional passengers. Looking at the entire network instead of individual stations will help you identify these opportunities.

As your network expands, transfers become increasingly important.

A successful transfer station allows passengers to reach many destinations without requiring every line to travel everywhere. Building a network of interconnected corridors is usually more efficient than creating a few extremely long routes.

Financial management remains just as important as engineering.

Always monitor your income, operating costs, and maintenance expenses before beginning major construction projects. A financially stable agency can continue expanding for decades, while one that spends too aggressively may struggle to recover.

The strongest transit systems are not necessarily the largest. They are the ones that provide fast, reliable, and convenient service to the greatest number of people while remaining financially sustainable.

You are now ready for the final tutorial, where you will learn about the long term goals of the simulation, future expansion, and what it takes to build a truly world class metropolitan transit network.

Tutorial Part 6: Your Transit Legacy

You now have everything you need to begin building your own transit network.

There is no single correct way to play DFW Regional Metroplex Simulation. Every decision you make shapes how the metropolitan area develops, how commuters travel, and how successful your agency becomes. Some players may focus on creating an extensive regional network, while others may prioritize profitability, efficiency, or connecting every major destination.

As your network grows, new challenges will continue to emerge. Passenger demand will increase, stations will become busier, and new opportunities for expansion will appear across the region. The best transit systems are never truly finished. They evolve over time as cities grow and travel patterns change.

Remember that every station, every rail line, and every train should serve a purpose. Expanding simply because you have enough money is rarely the best strategy. Instead, let demand guide your decisions. Study your statistics, identify weaknesses in your network, and invest where improvements will have the greatest impact.

Do not be discouraged if your first network is not perfect. Every successful transit agency has made costly mistakes, redesigned routes, and adjusted service as new challenges appeared. Learning from those decisions is part of the experience.

This project is also continually evolving.

Dallas Fort Worth is only the beginning. Future updates will introduce additional metropolitan areas, new transportation systems, expanded management mechanics, more policies, additional objectives, and many other features. Every update is designed to make the simulation more realistic and provide new ways to build and manage your transit agency.

Your feedback plays an important role in that process. If you discover a bug, have an idea for a new feature, or think something could be improved, please consider sharing it. Community feedback helps shape the future of the simulation.

Thank you for playing DFW Regional Metroplex Simulation. We hope you enjoy building your network, solving transportation challenges, and watching your city become more connected one station at a time.

Good luck, and welcome to the future of transit planning.
